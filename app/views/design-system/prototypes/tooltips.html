Surface water drainage strategy

You must show your development meets Buidling Regulations H external link.

This guidance includes:

  • checking infiltrations feasbility
  • giving preference to Soakaways. A soakaway is essentially a hole in the ground of your property's perimeter –usually the back garden – that is filled with rubble. Soakaways are designed to drain away excess water caused by heavy rainfall if the drainage system of your home struggles to keep up with demand.

Exception to soakways - where there is a history of groundwater flooding or a high water table where flows could re-emerge to flood lower levels or basements.

Surface water drainage design

This needs to meet Yorkshire Water standards. See information on sewers for adoption external link.

  • 50 per cent Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP) [have a pop out to explain what this is?] event
  • 3.33 per cent AEP event
  • 1 per cent event and climate change = no property flooding

Surface water flooding from events more than 3.33 per cent AEP can be within the site, including car parks and public open spaces. The associated risk needs to be managed so there is flooding on properties.

Discharge rates

For sites with less than 1 ha (what is a ha), a max discharge rate of 4 l/s can be used for all storms up to the 1per cent AEP event and climate change.

Alternatively, IH 124, ICP SUDS and FEH methods in ICOP can be used.

Brownfield

  • Minor development – a minimum 30 per cent reduction to peak flow rates up to the 1 per cent AEP event and climate change
  • Major development – if drainage survey can prove the existing site drains to a sewer or watercourse, a 50 per cent reduction can be applied (reduction of what?). If no proof, then Greenfield discharge rates will apply
Discharge rates

The existing discharge shall be the lesser of the capacity of the existing none sur charged pipe full capacity of the existing outfall, or can be taken as 140 l/s/ha of connected roofs and paving, provided that in both cases the existing drainage is still functional and point of discharge confirmed by Drainage survey.

For surface water drainage, there has to be no increase in flood risk off site, up to and during the critical 1 per cent Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP) event [link to Climate change???]

Flood mitigation measures are needed for all developments that are at risk of flooding.

The height of the measures will be based on the

  • design fluvial flood level
  • freeboard

Where sites are considered as ‘defended’, the developer shall make an assessment of the [this need a pop out explanation or hidden text]residual risk[pop out explanation] of flooding.

Residual risk

These need to have appropriate measures in drainage design including breach analysis, pumping station failure, culvert blockage, surface water conveyance system failure, safe access and egress).

Design fluvial flood level

This level is the 1 per cent Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP) and climate change modelled flood level.

See more information on the design fluvial flood level external link.

Freeboard

Freeboard is the height of the top of a bank, floodwall or other flood defence structure. This is above the design water level. This is a safety margin that also allows for uncertainties.

The freeboard above the design level flood for should be:

  • 300mm or more for industrial developments(?)
  • 300mm or more for underground car parks
  • 400mm or more for offices and commercial developents
  • 600mm or more for residential developments
